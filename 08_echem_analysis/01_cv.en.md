# Cyclic Voltammetry (CV) — Reversibility diagnosis and Randles-Sevcik analysis

## 1. Overview

Cyclic voltammetry (CV) records the current while sweeping the potential as a triangular wave, and uses the position, separation, and ratio of the oxidation and reduction peaks to diagnose reaction kinetics, reversibility, and mechanism in a single picture. Because a single measurement can simultaneously estimate the equilibrium potential, electron number, diffusion coefficient, and surface vs solution reaction, it has become the primary tool for new-material screening.

There are two key diagnostic variables: the separation $\Delta E_p$ between the oxidation and reduction peaks (reversibility) and the scan-rate dependence of the peak current, $i_p \propto \nu^{1/2}$ vs $i_p \propto \nu$ (diffusion vs surface behavior). Reading just these two variables accurately already completes a first-pass classification of the reaction.

## 2. Reversibility diagnosis: $\Delta E_p$ and $i_{pa}/i_{pc}$

Intuitively, if the electrode reaction is fast enough, oxidation and reduction follow nearly the equilibrium curve, so the oxidation and reduction peaks come very close. Conversely, if charge transfer is slow, more overpotential is required to drive the same current, so the two peaks separate. The Nicholson reversibility diagnostic quantifies this intuition.

For a **reversible** reaction at 25 °C:

$$\Delta E_p = E_{pa} - E_{pc} = \frac{59}{n} \text{ mV}, \qquad \frac{i_{pa}}{i_{pc}} = 1$$

Each term: $E_{pa}$ / $E_{pc}$ = oxidation / reduction peak potentials, $i_{pa}$ / $i_{pc}$ = oxidation / reduction peak currents, $n$ = number of electrons transferred. 59 mV for a one-electron reaction, about 30 mV for two-electron. In real measurements 60–70 mV is common owing to ohmic drop (IR drop) and instrument response, and once it exceeds 90 mV the reaction is classified as **quasi-reversible**.

In the quasi-reversible regime $\Delta E_p$ grows with scan rate $\nu$—the faster the scan, the more the kinetics fail to keep up, and the more the peaks separate. Quantifying this dependence with the Nicholson function $\psi(\Delta E_p)$ allows the standard rate constant $k^\circ$ to be extracted.

An **irreversible** reaction shows only one of the peaks ($i_{pa}/i_{pc} \neq 1$), or $\Delta E_p$ depends strongly on scan rate. EC and EC' mechanisms with coupled chemical steps are common causes.

> **Related concept: Butler-Volmer equation and the reversibility limit**
> Galvanostatic polarization of an electrode reaction: $j = j_0[\exp(\alpha F\eta/RT) - \exp(-(1-\alpha)F\eta/RT)]$.
> Each term: $j_0$ = exchange current density (equilibrium two-way flux), $\eta$ = activation overpotential, $\alpha$ = symmetry factor (typically 0.5).
> In CV the reversibility limit corresponds to large $\Lambda = k^\circ \sqrt{RT/(nFD\nu)}$ ($\Lambda > 15$). That is, when scan rate $\nu$ is sufficiently slow or $k^\circ$ is sufficiently large the reaction looks reversible; otherwise it appears quasi-reversible / irreversible. Note that the same system may appear irreversible at fast scan rates.

## 3. Randles-Sevcik equation: extracting the diffusion coefficient

For a reversible reaction the scan-rate dependence of the peak current is exactly $\nu^{1/2}$. Intuitively, a fast scan thins the diffusion boundary layer so a larger current flows; since the layer thickness scales as $\sqrt{Dt} \propto \nu^{-1/2}$, the current grows as $\nu^{1/2}$. The Randles-Sevcik equation quantifies this dependence.

The standard form for a reversible reaction at 25 °C:

$$i_p = (2.69 \times 10^5) \, n^{3/2} A D^{1/2} \nu^{1/2} c$$

Each term: $i_p$ = peak current (A), $n$ = number of electrons, $A$ = electrode area (cm²), $D$ = diffusion coefficient (cm²/s), $\nu$ = scan rate (V/s), $c$ = concentration of redox species (mol/cm³). Unit consistency is critical—plugging mm in place of cm or M in place of mol/cm³ throws the result off by more than an order of magnitude.

Experimental procedure: vary scan rate over roughly an order of magnitude (e.g., 5 mV/s, 10, 25, 50, 100, 200 mV/s) → plot $i_p$ vs $\nu^{1/2}$ → extract $D$ from the slope of the line. If the line is not clean, then (i) the reversibility assumption has broken (the system entered the quasi-reversible regime), (ii) surface reactions are mixed in, or (iii) the concentration is inaccurate.

Quasi-reversible reactions require Nicholson-Shain corrections, and irreversible reactions use the separate form $i_p = (2.99 \times 10^5) n (\alpha n_a)^{1/2} A D^{1/2} \nu^{1/2} c$. In other words, if you assume reversibility from the start and fit, you get an incorrect $D$.

## 4. Surface vs diffusion-controlled distinction

The case of an adsorbed species on the electrode surface reacting and the case of a species diffusing in from the solution have fundamentally different scan-rate dependencies. The amount of adsorbed species is finite and is fully consumed even as the scan rate increases, giving $i_p \propto \nu$, whereas a solution-diffusion reaction yields $i_p \propto \nu^{1/2}$ as shown above.

| Type | $i_p$ vs $\nu$ | $\Delta E_p$ | Peak shape |
|------|----------------|--------------|------------|
| Surface (adsorbed / thin film) | $i_p \propto \nu$ | 0 (in the ideal case) | symmetric, narrow |
| Diffusion (solution) | $i_p \propto \nu^{1/2}$ | 59/n mV | asymmetric, with tail |
| Mixed (composite / coating) | $i_p \propto \nu^x$ ($0.5 < x < 1$) | varies | intermediate |

For pseudocapacitor and thin-film electrodes, the slope $b$ of $\log i_p$ vs $\log \nu$ provides a quantitative diagnosis. $b = 0.5$ indicates diffusion control, $b = 1$ surface control, and intermediate values can be decomposed into a weighted sum of the two contributions (Dunn deconvolution).

Why this distinction matters for battery active-material analysis: the trade-off **fast power = surface contribution**, **high capacity = diffusion (bulk) contribution** is directly visible. Even for the same NMC particle, reducing the particle size increases the surface contribution and improves rate capability, but at the cost of volumetric energy density.

## 5. Multiple peaks and mechanism diagnosis

When several redox couples overlap at nearby potentials, separating them with a single CV is difficult; in such cases derivative CV ($di/dE$ vs $E$) or auxiliary techniques such as SECM and DEMS are needed. Multi-redox systems in battery cathodes (e.g., simultaneous Ni²⁺/³⁺/⁴⁺, Mn³⁺/⁴⁺) are a classic example.

When chemical steps are coupled to the CV, mechanisms are classified as EC, CE, or EC'. EC (electrochemical-chemical) shows the disappearance of the reduction peak as a chemical decomposition occurs after oxidation, and EC' (catalytic) shows an anomalously enlarged reduction peak as the chemical step regenerates the oxidized species. Pattern recognition alone narrows the candidate mechanisms.

## 6. Caveats during measurement

Quantitative interpretation of CV is highly sensitive to subtle differences in measurement conditions. The most common errors are:

- **Interpreting $\Delta E_p$ without iR correction**: if the electrolyte resistance is large (especially in non-aqueous or polymer systems) $\Delta E_p$ is overestimated. Apply positive feedback or post-processing correction.
- **Scan range too narrow**: the baseline before and after the peak must be visible to extract $i_p$ accurately. Typically peak position ± 200 mV is recommended.
- **First cycle vs steady cycle**: the first cycle is anomalous owing to SEI formation, surface oxidation, etc., so analysis usually uses the 2nd–3rd cycle.
- **AC noise / grounding**: insufficient shielding on BNC cables can mask small peaks. A Faraday cage is recommended.
- **Reference electrode drift**: if the reference potential drifts during long measurements, every peak position shifts together. Inspect via the OCV difference between start and end.

CV of battery active materials usually uses very slow scans of 0.1–1.0 mV/s—to allow time for bulk diffusion to follow. Faster than this, every system appears irreversible, which is why fast-scan CV is typically used for small-molecule redox or surface-adsorption diagnostics.
